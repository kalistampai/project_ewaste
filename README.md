***# Local Computer Pickup & Refurbishment: Free Marketing Playbook

> Practical, platform-by-platform strategies to source free computers for refurbishment and donation to kids — without spending money on ads.

## The Key Reality Check

Posting the same message “Bay Area-wide” feels efficient, but it backfires. Nextdoor, Craigslist, and Facebook actively suppress or ban accounts that blast identical posts across many neighborhoods. Mass-posting triggers shadow-bans and dramatically reduces responses.

**The faster, higher-converting approach:** Appear local and genuine in each community. Make small wording adjustments for each platform and neighborhood. This builds trust and consistently generates more calls.

## Platform-Specific Strategies

### Nextdoor — Your Highest-ROI Channel

Nextdoor is exceptionally effective because it is neighborhood-based, trust-oriented, and populated with exactly the homeowners who have a dead computer sitting in a closet.

**Recommended approach:**

- Create a free **Business Page** *and* post primarily from your **personal profile**. Personal posts receive significantly more reach than business posts.
- Post in the **“Free Items / Free Stuff”** and **“General”** categories — not under “Classifieds for sale.”
- Set posts to broadcast to **“Nearby neighborhoods”** (Nextdoor allows you to expand the radius). Avoid creating multiple fake accounts.
- Post once every **5–7 days**, rewording each time. Reply to every comment promptly — engagement pushes your post higher in feeds.
- Ask satisfied donors to leave a short comment such as *“Picked up — super easy!”* The algorithm rewards positive engagement, and visible social proof drives the next wave of responses.

### Craigslist

- Post in both **“community → volunteers”** and the **“free”** section. People browsing the free section are already in a decluttering mindset and ready to act.
- You can also frame the offer under **“services → skilled trades / computer”** as a free pickup service.
- Craigslist allows one post per city area every 48 hours per category. Rotate strategically: San Francisco, East Bay, South Bay, Peninsula, North Bay — one region per day. This gives legitimate coverage without triggering spam flags.
- Write your phone number in words (e.g., “call or text five-one-oh…”) to avoid scrapers and bots.

### Facebook Marketplace + Groups

Marketplace is designed for selling, so list your service under **“Free Stuff”** and include a photo of refurbished devices being given to children.

**The real leverage comes from local Facebook Groups:**

- Search and join the following types of groups:
  - `[Your City] Buy Nothing`
  - `[City] Free Stuff`
  - `[City] Community`
  - `Bay Area Freecycle`
  - Parent groups and senior groups (seniors often have the most old tech and the least experience disposing of it)
- Read each group’s rules carefully — many require admin approval before posting.
- Message the admin, explain the mission of giving refurbished computers to kids, and ask for permission. Admins frequently pin genuine charitable posts, giving you free premium placement.

## 10 Proven Free Marketing Tactics

1. **Buy Nothing Project groups**  
   Hyperlocal gift-economy communities are a natural fit. These members already give items away; your service simply removes the friction.

2. **Library & community center bulletin boards**  
   A clean half-page flyer with tear-off phone tabs works well. Libraries often support e-waste reduction and digital-literacy initiatives and will frequently allow you to leave a small stack.

3. **Schools & PTAs**  
   Email principals, PTA presidents, and school counselors. A message such as *“We provide refurbished computers to students who need them”* is commonly forwarded to parents. Ask to be included in the school newsletter — free and high reach.

4. **Faith communities**  
   Churches, mosques, temples, and synagogues maintain newsletters, bulletins, and announcement time. Framing the service as helping local children converts extremely well.

5. **Senior centers & HOAs**  
   Seniors commonly store old electronics and prefer speaking with a person rather than navigating a website. HOA newsletters and Nextdoor senior-focused groups are quiet, high-intent channels.

6. **Local businesses (coffee shops, laundromats, repair shops)**  
   Ask permission to leave flyers. Repair shops, in particular, regularly encounter customers whose devices are “not worth fixing” and can refer them directly to you.

7. **Word-of-mouth referral ask**  
   After every successful pickup, simply say:  
   > “If you know anyone else with old electronics, please send them my way — every device helps another child.”  
   This small, genuine ask compounds over time.

8. **Local Facebook & Reddit reuse communities**  
   Subreddits such as r/bayarea, r/sanfrancisco, and r/eastbay occasionally welcome community posts. Always follow the rules and post as a neighbor offering a helpful service rather than as an advertisement.

9. **Community events & farmers’ markets**  
   Bring a folding table, a clear sign, and a couple of refurbished laptops as proof. Collect devices on the spot and book pickups. Most events are free or low-cost to attend.

10. **Local press & neighborhood newsletters**  
    Email neighborhood blogs (Hoodline, Patch, local Substacks) with a short, feel-good angle: *“Local resident refurbishing e-waste to support kids.”* Reporters often appreciate positive community stories, and earned media is completely free.

**Quick Tips for Success**
- Always lead with the benefit to children or the community.
- Move fast on replies — responsiveness builds momentum.
- Track which neighborhoods and platforms produce the most pickups and double down on what works.
- Stay consistent: small, regular activity beats occasional big blasts.
