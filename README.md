First, one reality check that makes everything faster
"Bay Area-wide" posting is the instinct, but every platform (Nextdoor, Craigslist, Facebook) actively suppresses or bans accounts that blast identical posts across many areas. Ironically, mass-posting gets you fewer calls because you get shadow-banned. The fast-results move is to look local and genuine in each spot, with slight wording changes. That's what actually works.
Platform-by-Platform Steps
Nextdoor (your single best channel for this)
Nextdoor is gold here because it's neighborhood-based, trust-heavy, and full of exactly the homeowners with a dead PC in the closet.

Create a Business Page (free) AND post from your personal profile — personal posts get far more reach than business ones.
Post in the "Free Items" / "Free Stuff" and "General" categories, not "Classifieds for sale."
Set your post to broadcast to "Nearby neighborhoods" (Nextdoor lets you expand radius) rather than making 40 fake accounts.
Post once every 5–7 days, rewording each time. Reply to every comment fast — engagement pushes you up the feed.
Ask happy donors to comment "picked up, super easy" — Nextdoor's algorithm rewards that, and social proof drives the next callers.

Craigslist

Post under "community → volunteers" AND "free" — free-section browsers are decluttering-minded and ready to act.
Also try "services → skilled trades / computer" framed as free pickup.
Craigslist lets you post to one city area every 48 hours per category. Rotate: SF, East Bay, South Bay, Peninsula, North Bay — one region per day. That's legitimate coverage without triggering the "flagged for removal" mob.
Put your phone number written out ("call or text five-one-oh…") to dodge scrapers and bots.

Facebook Marketplace + Groups (groups beat Marketplace here)

Marketplace is built for selling, so list your service under "Free Stuff" with a photo of refurbished devices going to kids.
The real firepower is local Facebook Groups. Search and join: "[Your city] Buy Nothing," "[City] Free Stuff," "[City] Community," "Bay Area Freecycle," parent groups, senior groups (seniors have the most old tech and the least idea how to dump it).
Read each group's rules before posting — many require admin approval. Message the admin, explain the kids mission, and ask permission. Admins will often pin a genuine charitable post, which is free premium placement.

8–10 Proven Free Marketing Ideas

Buy Nothing Project groups — hyperlocal, gift-economy culture, and your service is a perfect fit. These people already give stuff away; you just make it effortless.
Library & community center bulletin boards — a clean half-page flyer with tear-off phone tabs. Libraries specifically love e-waste/literacy tie-ins and will often let you leave a stack.
Schools & PTAs — email principals, PTA presidents, and school counselors. "We give refurbished computers to students who need them" gets forwarded to parents instantly. Ask to be in the school newsletter (free, huge reach).
Faith communities — churches, mosques, temples, and synagogues have newsletters, bulletins, and announcement time. Framing: helping local kids. This converts extremely well.
Senior centers & HOAs — seniors hoard old electronics and prefer a person over a website. HOA newsletters and Nextdoor senior groups are quiet goldmines.
Local coffee shops, laundromats, repair shops — ask to leave flyers. Repair shops especially send you their "not worth fixing" customers.
Word-of-mouth referral ask — after every pickup, say: "If you know anyone else with old electronics, send them my way — every device helps another kid." Simple, and it compounds.
Local Facebook/Reddit reuse subs — r/bayarea, r/sanfrancisco, r/eastbay allow occasional community posts; check rules and post as a neighbor, not an ad.
Community events & farmers markets — a folding table, a sign, and a box of refurbished laptops as proof. Collect on the spot and book pickups. Free to attend most.
Local press & newsletters — email neighborhood blogs (Hoodline, Patch, local Substacks) a short "local guy refurbishing e-waste for kids" story. Reporters love a feel-good local angle and it's totally free.
